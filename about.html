The Splash of Lives

The Splash of Lives begins at the gate of St. Cross College of Technology on a burning-bright summer morning, when three freshers arrive with bags, doubts, excitement, and the strange feeling that life is about to become louder.

        What he finds at Splash is not just good filter coffee served in a steel tumbler the old Indiai way, but a table of regulars in their sixties who argue about road trips, quote things that stay with you, and listen the way people rarely do anymore. There is Ramachandra sir, loud and certain and completely joyful about it. There is Mrs. Sen, who speaks once an evening and lands with the precision of a grandmaster. There is Venkat sir himself — the room's quiet centre of gravity — who named the café after his wife, whose handwriting is on the poster by the door, and who waters the plant at a fresher's usual table a little more on the days he comes.

        Around this café, a life accumulates — a colleague brought in from a bad evening, a father's mistaken fury in a dark lane, a week at work that asked too much and gave too little, a dabba of rasam rice left on a kitchen counter by someone's mother who simply decided he needed feeding. None of it is dramatic. All of it is real.

        At its heart, The Splash of Lives is a story about a young man learning that belonging does not always announce itself, that the best things in a life are often found on a wrong turn, and that some rooms — once you have sat in them, ordered a second coffee, stayed until the lane outside turns amber — change the shape of everything outside them.

Written by Ayushman Gouda
